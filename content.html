What is art?

I like to think art is all around us. It can mean different things to different people, but ultimately, anything creative, self-made or creative can be art. It is very subjective, that’s why it is such an interesting topic to me.

What do I make and what is art like for me?

The art I mostly indulge in is digital drawing. I enjoy using highly saturated color palettes and bright values. I use applications such as Procreate and IbispaintX for my work. I have been doing it long-term for years at this point, starting around 10 years old, when I got my first device. I do it mostly for myself, but I recently have found enjoyment in trying out doing art for commission for clients online. Not only I get interesting ideas and suggestions for new drawings, but I also get paid now! It really has no downsides for me, maybe excluding the stress that comes with working for a client.

I actively engage in numerous online communities surrounding digital art. I have made friends and connections with plenty of other artists that inspire me to keep drawing. I also do a lot of collaborative works with my friends.

I also like to think any sort of creative output is good for one’s mental health. It’s nice to have an outlet like that. You can get inspired or touched by things in your life and then use the emotions you got to make something beautiful out of them. It’s therapeutic for me. Some say the way someone draws can be a window to their soul, and I agree. Someone’s style of choice, subject of their piece or even the supplies they use for it can really tell a lot about the author at times. But it is case-dependant, as no artist is ever the same as another.

Another thing I have noticed is that people’s approach to art can be very telling about how they perceive the world. For example, the best way to see someone’s relationship with art as a whole is to ask them a simple question regarding “modern” art. It’s very controversial nowadays and I love hearing what people are saying about it. Whether someone sees it as a money scheme, a social experiment or just a simple artwork really does show how they work in their head.

I have considered pursuing an actual career in digital arts, but for now, I am happy to keep it a safe, enjoyable activity for myself. From what I can tell, the current art-market is really unfriendly and money-obsessed. I have also heard that working conditions for artists have gotten a lot tougher in the recent years. I don’t want it to be like that for myself. It may change in the future, though. I don’t mind that. For the time being, I am satisfied with making a bit of money from commissions, but I do not work full time.

What about AI art?

A lot of people ask whether or not AI “art” is going to replace “real” artists. My answer? Absolutely not. Yes, it is cheaper to ask a chatbot to make you an image, but the result is a lot more soulless, artificial. Some people may not care, but I know that there is a big group of people who do. Even if AI makes a really realistic and human-like portrait, there’s no message, no feelings in it. There’s no sweat and tears that went into the process. And those things matter to people too. If I had purchased an artwork that was deeply personal, filled to the brim with emotions of the author, I’d feel connected to it, grateful to have it. You just don’t get that sort of connection with artificial graphics.

A lot of people argue that AI makes art “more accessible”, especially to those with disabilities or difficulties learning it. I disagree wholeheartedly. There are so many artists out there who make beautiful, astonishing pieces regardless of their disabilities. They spend years learning to perfect the process, they need more time to work around their struggles, but it all really just adds more soul and beauty to the piece, in my opinion. I think it’s beautiful that someone can try so hard to achieve something, even if struggling.
